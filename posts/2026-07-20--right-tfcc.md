i injured my TFCC in February 2026. there were omens beforehand: positive ulnar deviance and brief bouts of tennis elbow. 
the acute moment of injury was not actually from climbing, but from falling off my scooter on Mass Ave Bridge on my way home from a vc pizza party.
but i want to say that the injury has everything to do with climbing, bar the moment it happened: it had been only a couple months since my first time climbing and I was pushing myself on training boards, climbing three times a week (often two days in a row), and never warming up.

the injury presented with sharp pain on the ulnar side of my right wrist during extension, pronation, and supination, and at its worst I could barely twist a doorknob or hold a plank position. two months later, I was able to return to climbing at a similar intensity pre-injury. five months later, I have completely forgotten the injury has ever happened.

here is a list of things I did during that time, ranked from least to most helpful:

**ignoring it and climbing** - I thought hopping on some easy climbs a week after the injury would be okay, nope. any sort of undercling, gaston, or sloper led to immediate pain feedback, and I would wake up the next morning with a worse enough wrist that I was certain that I had stunted my recovery. not recommended.

**wrist brace/taping** - for around two weeks early in my injury, I experimented with wearing a wrist brace or taping my wrist for the majority of my waking hours, forcibly limiting my range of motion on the pain-causing movements. early enough in the injury I believe this to be okay, especially if the pain you experience during everyday movements is genuinely affecting you. however I recommend against this as a long-term solution. the forcibly limited range of motion doesn't provide enough stimuli to the wrist complex to encourage its growth, and also hinders your mental assessment of your injury through the pain feedback. also, taping my wrist with enough tension to be useful often ended up affecting blood flow to my fingers after extended duration. I've heard wrist widgets or other, less inhibiting, support is more helpful for recovery.

**rehab exercise** - I got these from my physio but have also seen very similar information online. my routine every day was wrist flexion, wrist extension, pronation/supination, and ulnar deviation, 12 reps of each in a set and 3 sets total. most days I used a yellow resistance band (5-15 lbs) and when I had access I used anything from a 5 lb to 10 lb dumbbell depending on the progress of my injury. I feel like these were genuinely the only helpful thing for my recovery, and I still do them as part of my warm up before every climb session, and my wrists have felt great since. I find the routine to be not too boring but still encompassing enough for overall TFCC support.

**leg days** - here's where I found my TFCC injury to be a blessing in disguise. because I was so far away from any sort of pulling, I invested a lot of time into my legs while I was injured, in terms of both mobility and strength. Leg workouts kept me eating well and motivated to hit the gym, and mobility work translated to genuine on-the wall gains. I even went to the climbing gym to make up some no-hands problems on the slab wall. During this time I progressed to 5x3 pistol squats from 1 rep on each side, learned to touch my toes, and developed a thorough and personally helpful leg mobility routine that I still use on rest days now. When an injury just makes being on the wall impossible, you have to find other ways to stay moving. I genuinely believe that if I didn't take the time to work on my legs and make stuff up, I might never have returned to climbing with the amount of time I took off the wall and the motivation I would have lost.
